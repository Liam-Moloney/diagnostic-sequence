Early Symptoms

The problem began quietly. In my mid-twenties I developed persistent pain in my feet while working long shifts in a bar. At first it seemed like an ordinary physical strain. The pain appeared and disappeared and was treated as a local foot issue. Over the years I saw chiropodists and general practitioners for what appeared to be common problems such as corns or calluses.

But the symptoms never fully disappeared. They evolved slowly, becoming harder to explain and increasingly difficult to live with.

Loss of Function

By the mid-2010s the problem had become more serious. Walking had changed in ways that were difficult to describe. My gait became stiff and abnormal, and the pain in my feet became severe enough to interfere with daily life and work.

In 2017 the situation reached a point where I could no longer continue working normally. My GP referred me urgently to neurology for assessment of a possible neurological disorder.

The Hospital Admission

In July 2017 I was admitted to University Hospital Galway for neurological evaluation. Over the course of the week numerous examinations and tests were carried out. During this admission a working diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease was introduced very early in the process.

The hospital carried out imaging and clinical assessments, and a dopamine transporter scan was scheduled. However, a key diagnostic step commonly used in suspected Parkinsonian conditions — a therapeutic trial of dopaminergic medication — was deliberately postponed.

At the time this decision was presented as a delay in treatment rather than as part of the diagnostic process.

Discharge

Shortly before discharge I raised a direct concern. The severe pain and dysfunction in my feet — the problem that had originally led to the referral — had not been explained or resolved. I asked how I could be discharged when the original issue remained unanswered.

Despite this, the discharge went ahead.

On 11 July 2017 I left the hospital with a diagnosis of idiopathic Parkinson’s disease recorded in my medical file. The diagnosis was also formalised through the Long Term Illness scheme, a statutory programme used in Ireland to certify certain chronic conditions and support long-term treatment.

Life Under the Diagnosis

Following discharge my treatment continued along the standard Parkinson’s pathway. Medications were prescribed and adjusted over time as clinicians attempted to manage the condition according to established Parkinson’s treatment frameworks.

Yet aspects of the illness never fully aligned with the diagnosis. In particular, a medication called procyclidine produced an unusually strong improvement in symptoms, including the severe foot pain that had existed for years. When the medication was later withdrawn, the symptoms returned almost immediately.

Despite these unusual features the diagnostic framework remained unchanged for years.

A Delayed Explanation

Nearly nine years later a genetic investigation finally provided a clear answer. Specialists confirmed that I carry two pathogenic variants in the PRKN gene, establishing a monogenic cause of Parkinsonism and dystonia.

This discovery did not introduce a new illness. Instead, it provided the underlying explanation that had remained unresolved since the events of 2017.

Why the Case Matters

The significance of this case does not lie simply in whether a diagnosis was correct or incorrect. Medicine frequently works through uncertainty and evolving evidence.

What makes this case notable is the order in which events occurred. A diagnostic conclusion became administratively fixed while elements of the clinical inquiry were still incomplete. Once that conclusion entered records and administrative systems it became increasingly difficult to revisit or reopen.

The documents and analysis presented in this project examine that sequence of events in detail.
