Structural conclusions emerging from the sequence of events documented in this archive.

1. Diagnostic Finality Occurred Before Inquiry Was Complete

During the July 2017 hospital admission a diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease was recorded and administratively formalised before the full diagnostic inquiry had been completed.

Key elements of the presenting clinical problem — particularly the severe foot-centred symptoms that had triggered referral — remained unresolved at the point of discharge.

The sequence of events therefore shows diagnostic finality attaching while inquiry was still incomplete.

2. Administrative Processes Stabilised the Diagnosis

Once the diagnosis entered administrative systems — including statutory certification and medical records — it began to function as an established fact within the clinical file.

Subsequent clinical activity took place largely within that established framework, creating a stable narrative that persisted for years.

3. A Contemporaneous Protest Was Not Reflected in the Record

Shortly before discharge the unresolved nature of the initiating symptoms was raised directly on the ward during a teaching round attended by clinical staff and students.

This event does not appear in the discharge documentation that followed the admission.

As a result the official record does not fully capture the context in which the diagnostic closure occurred.

4. Governance Processes Began Years Later

Formal questions about the diagnostic sequence were first raised through institutional complaint procedures years after the original admission.

By that time the diagnostic framework established in 2017 had been embedded within medical records and treatment pathways for an extended period.

5. Later Genetic Testing Clarified the Underlying Condition

In 2026 genetic testing identified pathogenic variants in the PRKN gene, establishing a monogenic cause of Parkinsonism and dystonia.

This finding provided the causal explanation that had remained unresolved since the original admission.
